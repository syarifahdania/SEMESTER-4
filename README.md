# Year 2, Semester 2 · Session 2024/2025

---

## 🚀 Technopreneurship Seminar · SECD3761

This seminar introduced the business and entrepreneurial dimension of technology — exploring how technical capability translates into products, ventures, and commercial value. The business model canvas, value proposition design, MVP thinking, and pitch development formed the core of a curriculum that pulled me outside my purely technical frame of reference.

The most lasting shift was learning to evaluate a technical idea not only by whether it can be built, but by whether it solves a real problem for real people in a sustainable way. That question — "for whom and to what end?" — now accompanies every project I undertake, academic or otherwise. The ability to communicate a technical vision to a non-technical audience is something I continue to develop, because it is the bridge between building something and having it matter.

## 🧱 Object Oriented Programming · SECJ2154

Object-oriented programming introduced a design philosophy that has since become the default lens through which I architect software. Classes, encapsulation, inheritance, polymorphism, and abstraction were not just new syntax — they represented a different way of thinking about how code should be organised and how complexity should be managed.

The real understanding came from experiencing what poor encapsulation costs. Bugs that cascade across a codebase because internal state was exposed unnecessarily are among the most frustrating to trace, and encountering them firsthand made the principles feel earned rather than prescribed. By the end of the course, I could read unfamiliar codebases much more confidently — understanding their structure before understanding their detail — which is a skill that scales well into any professional codebase.

## 🔍 Data Mining · SECP2753

Data mining sits at the intersection of statistics, computing, and domain knowledge, and this course covered it comprehensively — preprocessing, classification, clustering, association rule mining, and model evaluation formed a complete analytical workflow that I now use as a professional reference point.

Implementing algorithms from first principles before using library abstractions was deliberately demanding, and deliberately valuable. I developed genuine intuition for what these methods are doing mathematically, rather than treating them as black boxes. The most practically important insight was about data preprocessing: in real-world projects, cleaning and preparing data accounts for the majority of the work, because the quality of every downstream output is determined entirely by the quality of the input. That understanding informs how I approach every data pipeline I design.

## 📊 Data Analytics Programming · SECP3223

This was the course where my degree felt most directly connected to my professional ambitions. Working in Python with Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, and Seaborn to clean, explore, and visualise real datasets turned data engineering from an abstract career path into something I could see myself doing every day.

The visualisation component of the course was as important as the analytical one. The best analysis is wasted if it cannot be communicated clearly to someone who was not in the room when the data was processed. Learning to build visualisations that tell a story — rather than simply displaying numbers — changed how I think about the final mile of any data project. I want to scale these skills to larger datasets using PySpark and develop stronger habits around reproducible analysis using well-structured notebooks and version-controlled environments.

## 🖥️ Operating Systems · SECR2043

Operating systems govern everything that happens on a computer — process scheduling, memory management, file systems, and hardware abstraction — and this course revealed the elegance of that governance in detail. The algorithms behind process scheduling, virtual memory, and deadlock prevention were far more sophisticated than I had assumed.

Understanding how the OS allocates and arbitrates resources changed how I write code. I became more conscious of memory usage, more aware of concurrency risks, and more thoughtful about how my programs behave at the system level rather than just at the application level. For a data engineer working with distributed systems and large-scale data processing, this kind of systems literacy is not peripheral — it is directly applicable to diagnosing and preventing performance issues in production environments.

## 🗣️ Professional Communication Skills 1 · UHLB2122

This course built the writing and presentation skills that technical education often leaves to chance. Structured technical reports, formal presentations, and professional correspondence formed the curriculum, with feedback cycles that made improvement visible and measurable.

The central lesson — that understanding something and communicating it clearly are two entirely separate skills — was more significant than I anticipated. Tailoring technical content for different audiences, knowing what to include and what to leave out, and structuring a presentation so that its logic is apparent without explanation: these are capabilities that determine whether technical work has impact beyond the person who produced it. I continue to develop my ability to communicate complex analytical work clearly and persuasively, because it is one of the highest-value professional skills I can hold.

---

## 💭 Philosophy and Current Issues · ULRS1022

Philosophy introduced a mode of thinking distinct from everything else in a computing curriculum — slower, more questioning, and more concerned with justification than with solution. Critical thinking, ethical theory, and structured argumentation applied to contemporary issues formed a course that was genuinely different from anything else I studied.

The most directly applicable outcome was developing the habit of questioning assumptions — including my own. In a field that moves quickly and rewards decisiveness, the philosophical instinct to ask "but is this right, and for whom?" is a counterweight that I now consider professionally essential. Applied to questions of AI bias, data privacy, and algorithmic accountability, this is not abstract thinking — it is the kind of reasoning that prevents real harm.
